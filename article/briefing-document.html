Briefing Document: The Composition, Function, and Systems of Human Blood

Executive Summary

This document provides a comprehensive analysis of human blood, a vital connective tissue responsible for transport, defense, and regulation within the body. Blood consists of two primary components: plasma, the liquid matrix, and formed elements, which include erythrocytes (red blood cells), leukocytes (white blood cells), and platelets. Plasma, which constitutes approximately 55% of blood volume, is primarily water but is rich in essential proteins—albumin, globulins, and fibrinogen—that regulate osmotic pressure, transport lipids, facilitate immunity, and enable clotting. The formed elements comprise the remaining 45%, a value known as the hematocrit.

Erythrocytes are the most numerous cells, specialized for oxygen transport via hemoglobin. Their production is regulated by the hormone erythropoietin from the kidneys. Leukocytes are the cornerstone of the immune system, with five distinct types (neutrophils, eosinophils, basophils, lymphocytes, and monocytes) that specialize in defending against pathogens through processes like phagocytosis and antibody production. Platelets are cellular fragments critical for hemostasis, the multi-phase process of stopping blood flow after an injury, which involves vascular spasm, the formation of a platelet plug, and a complex coagulation cascade that culminates in a stable fibrin clot.

Blood typing, based on the ABO and Rh antigen systems on the surface of erythrocytes, is crucial for safe blood transfusions. The presence or absence of these antigens determines a person's blood type and the corresponding antibodies in their plasma, defining compatibility between donors and recipients. Understanding these interconnected systems is fundamental to both physiology and clinical medicine.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

I. Overview of Blood Composition

Blood is classified as a type of connective tissue, unique in that its cells are suspended in a liquid extracellular matrix known as plasma. When whole blood is separated in a centrifuge, it visibly stratifies into three distinct layers based on density.

  • Plasma: The uppermost, straw-colored liquid layer, constituting about 55% of the total blood volume.
  • Buffy Coat: A thin, whitish layer found between the plasma and red blood cells. It contains leukocytes and platelets and makes up about 1% of the total volume.
  • Erythrocytes (Red Blood Cells): The densest component, settling at the bottom. This layer represents the hematocrit, or the percentage of blood volume occupied by red blood cells, which is normally around 45%.

Key Functions of Blood

The functions of blood fall into three primary categories:

Category

Description of Functions

Transport

Delivers oxygen from the lungs and nutrients from the digestive tract to all body cells. Transports metabolic waste products from cells to elimination sites (lungs and kidneys). Carries hormones from endocrine organs to their target tissues.

Defense

Protects against blood loss through clotting mechanisms involving platelets and plasma proteins. Defends against foreign invaders like bacteria and viruses, with white blood cells serving as the primary agents of the immune system.

Regulation

Maintains appropriate body temperature by absorbing and distributing heat. Regulates normal pH in body tissues using buffer systems. Maintains adequate fluid volume and osmotic balance in the circulatory system.

II. Plasma: The Liquid Matrix

Plasma is the liquid portion of blood, a fluid connective tissue that is approximately 90-91% water. The remaining 9-10% consists of a multitude of dissolved substances, with plasma proteins being the most abundant.

Plasma Proteins and Their Roles

Plasma proteins, which are not typically used for energy, are primarily produced by the liver (with the exception of gamma globulins) and have diverse, critical functions.

  1. Albumin: The most abundant plasma protein, albumin is crucial for maintaining the osmotic balance (osmotic pressure) between the blood and tissue fluids. This regulation is essential for managing blood volume and blood pressure.
  2. Globulins: This is a diverse group of proteins categorized into three main types:
    • Alpha and Beta Globulins: Produced by the liver, these proteins act as transporters for lipids and other substances. Since lipids like cholesterol and triglycerides are hydrophobic and cannot travel easily in water-based plasma, they are enveloped by these proteins to form complexes called lipoproteins. Examples include High-Density Lipoproteins (HDL), Low-Density Lipoproteins (LDL), and Very-Low-Density Lipoproteins (VLDL). High levels of LDL are associated with arterial disease.
    • Gamma Globulins: These proteins are also known as antibodies or immunoglobulins. Unlike other plasma proteins, they are produced by B lymphocytes (a type of white blood cell) and are essential components of the immune system, defending the body against pathogens.
  3. Fibrinogen: Produced by the liver, fibrinogen is a soluble protein that is a key precursor in the blood clotting process. When a blood vessel is damaged, it is converted into an insoluble protein called fibrin, which forms the structural mesh of a blood clot.

Other Transported Substances in Plasma

Beyond proteins, plasma carries a wide array of other essential and waste substances:

  • Nutrients: Products of digestion, such as glucose, amino acids, nucleotides, and lipids, are transported throughout the body.
  • Gases: While red blood cells carry most oxygen, plasma carries a small amount of dissolved oxygen and a significant portion of carbon dioxide, primarily in the form of bicarbonate ions (HCO₃⁻).
  • Non-protein Nitrogenous Substances: These are metabolic waste products, including urea and uric acid, which are transported from cells to the kidneys for filtration and excretion.
  • Electrolytes: These are salts (ions like sodium, potassium, and calcium) that are vital for maintaining blood pH, osmotic balance, neuron function, and muscle contraction.

III. The Formed Elements: Cellular Components

The formed elements are the cellular and cell-fragment components of blood, all of which originate from a common stem cell, the hemocytoblast, in the red bone marrow.

A. Erythrocytes (Red Blood Cells)

Erythrocytes (RBCs) are the most numerous blood cells, with an average of 5 to 6 million cells per cubic millimeter in males.

  • Structure and Function: RBCs are small, biconcave discs. This shape maximizes the surface area for gas diffusion. Critically, mature RBCs are anucleated (lack a nucleus) and have few organelles. This allows them to carry more hemoglobin—approximately 300 million molecules per cell—and provides the flexibility needed to squeeze through narrow capillaries. Their primary function is transporting oxygen, which binds to the iron within the hemoglobin molecule. Oxygenated hemoglobin (oxyhemoglobin) gives blood its bright red color.
  • Life Cycle and Regulation: RBCs have a lifespan of about 120 days (or 3-4 months) due to the wear and tear of circulating through capillaries without a nucleus to direct repairs. Their production, known as erythropoiesis, is regulated by the hormone erythropoietin (EPO), which is released by the kidneys in response to low blood oxygen levels. Old or damaged RBCs are removed from circulation by phagocytic white blood cells in the liver and spleen. Hemoglobin is broken down: the globin protein is recycled into amino acids, and the iron-containing heme group is converted into a pigment called bilirubin, which the liver processes and excretes. A buildup of bilirubin can cause jaundice.

B. Leukocytes (White Blood Cells)

Leukocytes (WBCs) are complete cells with a nucleus and organelles, functioning primarily in immune defense. They make up only about 1% of blood volume but are critical for protecting the body. Unlike RBCs, they can move autonomously and leave the bloodstream to enter tissues to fight infections, a process guided by chemical signals (chemotaxis). There are five types of leukocytes, categorized as granulocytes or agranulocytes.

C. Platelets (Thrombocytes)

Platelets are not true cells but are fragments of cytoplasm that bud off from extremely large cells in the red bone marrow called megakaryocytes. With a normal count between 130,000 and 360,000 per microliter, their primary function is to initiate the process of hemostasis.

IV. Hemostasis: The Process of Blood Clotting

Hemostasis is the rapid, localized, and carefully controlled process of stopping bleeding. It occurs in three sequential phases.

  1. Phase 1: Vascular Spasm (Vasoconstriction): Immediately following injury to a blood vessel, the surrounding smooth muscle contracts, causing the vessel to constrict. This vasospasm reduces blood flow and minimizes blood loss, lasting for a few minutes.
  2. Phase 2: Platelet Plug Formation: When the vessel is damaged, underlying collagen fibers are exposed. Circulating platelets adhere to these fibers and become "sticky," releasing chemicals that attract more platelets to the site. This aggregation forms a temporary platelet plug, which is effective in sealing small breaks.
  3. Phase 3: Coagulation (Blood Clotting): This is the most effective phase and involves a complex cascade of enzymatic reactions using clotting factors.
    • Damaged tissues release chemicals (like thromboplastin) that lead to the formation of prothrombin activator.
    • In the presence of calcium ions (Ca²⁺), prothrombin activator converts the plasma protein prothrombin into the active enzyme thrombin.
    • Thrombin then catalyzes the conversion of the soluble plasma protein fibrinogen into long, insoluble threads of fibrin.
    • These fibrin threads form a mesh that traps red blood cells and platelets, creating a stable blood clot that seals the vessel until it can be repaired.

An abnormal clot that forms in an unbroken blood vessel is called a thrombus. If this clot dislodges and travels through the bloodstream, it is called an embolus, which can lodge in a critical vessel and cause a stroke or heart attack.

V. Human Blood Groups and Transfusion

The surfaces of red blood cells are marked by genetically determined proteins and glycoproteins called antigens. These antigens provoke an immune response if they are foreign to the body. The presence or absence of specific antigens is the basis for blood typing.

A. The ABO System

This system is based on the presence of two antigens: A and B. A person's plasma contains pre-formed antibodies (agglutinins) against the antigens that are absent on their own RBCs.

If incompatible blood types are mixed, the recipient's antibodies bind to the donor's antigens, causing the red blood cells to clump together, a process called agglutination. This can be fatal.

B. The Rh System

The Rh blood group is based on the presence or absence of the Rh antigen (also called the D antigen), first discovered in Rhesus monkeys.

  • Rh-positive (Rh+): The Rh antigen is present on the RBCs.
  • Rh-negative (Rh-): The Rh antigen is absent. Unlike the ABO system, anti-Rh antibodies are not pre-formed in Rh- individuals; they are only produced after exposure to Rh+ blood.

C. Transfusion Compatibility

Combining both systems determines full compatibility:

  • Universal Donor: Type O-negative blood lacks all A, B, and Rh antigens, making it the safest to give to any recipient in an emergency.
  • Universal Recipient: Type AB-positive blood has all A, B, and Rh antigens, meaning the recipient's plasma has no antibodies to attack any donor blood type.

D. Clinical Considerations: Erythroblastosis Fetalis

This condition, also known as hemolytic disease of the newborn, can occur when an Rh-negative mother carries an Rh-positive fetus. During childbirth, fetal Rh+ blood can enter the mother's circulation, causing her to produce anti-Rh antibodies. If she becomes pregnant with a second Rh+ fetus, these maternal antibodies can cross the placenta and attack the fetus's red blood cells, causing severe anemia and potential harm. This is now preventable with an injection (RhoGAM) that prevents the mother from forming the antibodies.

E. Genetics of Blood Types

The ABO blood types are determined by a single gene with three alleles: Iᴬ (A), Iᴮ (B), and i (O).

  • Iᴬ and Iᴮ are codominant, meaning if both are present (genotype IᴬIᴮ), the person has type AB blood.
  • Both Iᴬ and Iᴮ are dominant over the recessive i allele.
  • A person with type O blood must have the homozygous recessive genotype (ii).
