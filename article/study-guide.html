Blood and Its Components: A Comprehensive Study Guide

This guide provides a detailed review of the components and functions of blood, covering plasma, formed elements, blood typing, and hemostasis. It is designed to test and reinforce understanding of these critical topics in human biology.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Quiz: Short Answer Questions

Answer the following questions in 2-3 sentences each, based on the provided source material.

  1. Describe the composition of blood plasma after centrifugation.
  2. What are the three main groups of plasma proteins, and what is the primary function of each?
  3. Explain the process of hemostasis, listing its three main phases.
  4. What are antigens and antibodies in the context of the ABO blood group system?
  5. Why is Type O negative blood considered the "universal donor"?
  6. What is the role of the hormone erythropoietin (EPO), which organ produces it, and what stimulates its release?
  7. Differentiate between granulocytes and agranulocytes, and list the five types of leukocytes.
  8. Explain the process of phagocytosis as performed by specific leukocytes.
  9. Describe the genetic basis of the ABO blood types, including the concepts of dominance, recessiveness, and codominance.
  10. What is erythroblastosis fetalis and how does it relate to the Rh factor?

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

Answer Key

  1. When whole blood is centrifuged, plasma separates as the top layer, constituting about 55% of the blood's volume. It is a straw-colored or yellowish liquid that is approximately 90-91% water. The remaining portion consists of dissolved substances, including plasma proteins, nutrients, salts (electrolytes), hormones, and waste products like urea.
  2. The three main groups of plasma proteins are albumins, globulins, and fibrinogen. Albumins, the most abundant, are produced by the liver and maintain osmotic balance. Globulins are diverse; alpha and beta globulins transport lipids, while gamma globulins (antibodies) are crucial for immunity. Fibrinogen is a soluble protein that is converted to insoluble fibrin to form blood clots.
  3. Hemostasis is the process of stopping bleeding, which occurs in three phases. The first phase is a blood vessel spasm (vasoconstriction), where the smooth muscle of a damaged vessel contracts to reduce blood loss. This is followed by the formation of a platelet plug, where platelets adhere to exposed collagen and each other. The final and most effective phase is blood coagulation, the formation of a fibrin clot that traps blood cells and seals the wound.
  4. Antigens are proteins or glycoproteins found on the surface of red blood cells that determine blood type, such as the A and B antigens in the ABO system. Antibodies (or immunoglobulins) are defensive proteins produced by lymphocytes that circulate in the plasma. They are specific to foreign antigens and will attack red blood cells that have antigens not native to the body, causing agglutination.
  5. Type O negative blood is considered the universal donor because its red blood cells have no A, B, or Rh surface antigens. Consequently, a recipient's immune system will not have any corresponding antibodies (like anti-A, anti-B, or anti-Rh) that would recognize these cells as foreign. This prevents an agglutination reaction, making it safe for transfusion into individuals of any ABO or Rh blood type.
  6. Erythropoietin (EPO) is a hormone that stimulates the red bone marrow to increase the production of red blood cells. It is produced by the kidneys in response to low oxygen levels in the blood. This creates a negative feedback loop: low oxygen triggers EPO release, which increases red blood cell count and oxygen-carrying capacity, which in turn inhibits further EPO production.
  7. Leukocytes are classified based on the presence of granules in their cytoplasm. Granulocytes, which include neutrophils, eosinophils, and basophils, have visible granules. Agranulocytes, which include lymphocytes and monocytes, lack these visible granules.
  8. Phagocytosis is a process where a cell engulfs foreign invaders or cellular debris. Neutrophils and monocytes are the primary phagocytes; they extend their cytoplasm to surround a pathogen (like bacteria), enclose it within their membrane, and then use internal lysosomes to digest and destroy it. When monocytes leave the bloodstream, they mature into even larger phagocytes called macrophages.
  9. The ABO blood type is determined by three alleles: A, B, and O (often written as I). The A and B alleles are codominant, meaning if an individual has the AB genotype, both A and B antigens are expressed. The O allele is recessive to both A and B, so a person must have the OO genotype to have type O blood. Genotypes AA or AO result in type A blood, and genotypes BB or BO result in type B blood.
  10. Erythroblastosis fetalis is a condition that can occur when an Rh-negative mother carries an Rh-positive fetus. If fetal blood enters the mother's circulation (often during the first birth), her immune system develops anti-Rh antibodies. In a subsequent pregnancy with another Rh-positive fetus, these maternal antibodies can cross the placenta and attack the fetus's red blood cells, causing severe anemia and potential harm.

Glossary of Key Terms

Term

Definition

ABO System

The most common system for blood typing, based on the presence or absence of A and B antigens on the surface of red blood cells.

Agglutination

The clumping of red blood cells that occurs when antibodies in the plasma bind to corresponding antigens on the cells' surface.

Agranulocytes

A category of white blood cells that lack visible granules in their cytoplasm. This group includes lymphocytes and monocytes.

Albumin

The most abundant plasma protein, produced by the liver, which helps maintain the osmotic balance and pressure between blood and tissue fluid.

Anemia

A disorder characterized by a lower-than-normal hematocrit or number of red blood cells, leading to reduced oxygen-carrying capacity.

Antibody

A Y-shaped defensive protein, also known as an immunoglobulin or gamma globulin, produced by B lymphocytes that circulates in the plasma and targets specific foreign antigens.

Anticoagulant

A chemical substance, such as CPD (citric phosphate dextrose), that prevents blood from clotting.

Antigen

A protein or glycoprotein on the surface of a cell (e.g., a red blood cell) that can invoke an immune response, particularly the generation of antibodies.

Basophil

The rarest type of granulocytic white blood cell, which stains dark blue and releases histamine to promote inflammation and heparin to inhibit clotting at the site of damaged tissue.

Bilirubin

A yellow-orange pigment that is the final product of the breakdown of the heme portion of hemoglobin. It is processed by the liver and gives color to feces and urine.

Blood Vessel Spasm

The first phase of hemostasis, where the smooth muscle in the wall of a damaged blood vessel contracts (vasoconstriction) to reduce blood loss.

Buffy Coat

A thin, whitish layer that appears between the red blood cells and the plasma after centrifugation of blood. It contains the white blood cells and platelets.

Chemotaxis

The movement of a cell, such as a white blood cell, toward or away from a chemical stimulus, such as chemicals released by bacteria.

Coagulation

The third and most effective phase of hemostasis; the process of blood clotting, which involves a complex cascade of reactions culminating in the formation of a fibrin mesh.

Codominance

A genetic scenario where two different alleles for a trait are both fully expressed in the phenotype, as seen with the A and B alleles in AB blood type.

Embolus

A blood clot (thrombus) that has dislodged from its original location and is traveling through the bloodstream.

Eosinophil

A type of granulocytic white blood cell that stains bright red and is primarily involved in responding to allergic reactions and fighting parasitic worm infections.

Erythroblastosis Fetalis

A potentially fatal condition in a fetus or newborn (also called hemolytic disease of the newborn) where an Rh-negative mother's antibodies attack the red blood cells of her Rh-positive child.

Erythrocyte

A red blood cell; a biconcave, anucleated disc containing approximately 300 million hemoglobin molecules specialized for transporting oxygen.

Erythropoietin (EPO)

A hormone produced by the kidneys in response to low oxygen levels that stimulates the red bone marrow to produce more red blood cells.

Fibrin

An insoluble, thread-like protein that forms a mesh network to trap blood cells and platelets, creating a stable blood clot.

Fibrinogen

A soluble plasma protein that is the precursor to fibrin. It is converted to fibrin by the enzyme thrombin during blood coagulation.

Globulins

A group of plasma proteins. Alpha and beta globulins transport lipids and are produced by the liver. Gamma globulins (antibodies) are produced by lymphocytes for immune defense.

Granulocytes

A category of white blood cells characterized by the presence of visible granules in their cytoplasm. This group includes neutrophils, eosinophils, and basophils.

Hematocrit

The percentage of the total blood volume that is composed of red blood cells, normally around 45%.

Hemocytoblast

The stem cell found in red bone marrow from which all formed elements of the blood (red cells, white cells, and platelets) originate.

Hemoglobin

The iron-containing protein within red blood cells, composed of four polypeptide chains, that reversibly binds to and transports oxygen.

Hemostasis

The physiological process that stops bleeding from a damaged blood vessel. It involves vasospasm, platelet plug formation, and coagulation.

Histamine

A chemical released by basophils and mast cells that causes blood vessels to dilate, promoting inflammation.

Leukemia

A cancer of the blood-forming tissues, characterized by the abnormal and excessive production of immature, non-functional white blood cells.

Leukocyte

A white blood cell; the only true, complete cells in the blood, responsible for defending the body against pathogens and disease.

Lipoproteins

Complexes of lipids surrounded by a protein shell that allow for the transport of hydrophobic lipids (like cholesterol and triglycerides) in the aqueous plasma. Examples include HDL and LDL.

Lymphocyte

A type of agranulocytic white blood cell responsible for specific immunity. B lymphocytes produce antibodies, and they are long-lived, providing long-term immunity.

Macrophage

A large phagocytic cell derived from a monocyte that has left the bloodstream. Its name means "big eater," and it engulfs pathogens and cellular debris.

Megakaryocyte

A large cell with a giant nucleus found in the red bone marrow, from which platelets are formed as fragments of its cytoplasm bud off.

Monocyte

The largest type of agranulocytic white blood cell, which functions as a phagocyte and can leave the bloodstream to become a macrophage in the tissues.

Neutrophil

The most abundant type of granulocytic white blood cell, which acts as a primary phagocyte, engulfing and destroying bacteria.

Phagocytosis

The process by which a cell engulfs solid particles, such as bacteria or cellular debris, to destroy them.

Plasma

The liquid matrix of blood, making up about 55% of its volume. It is mostly water and contains dissolved proteins, nutrients, hormones, and waste products.

Platelet

Also known as a thrombocyte; a small, anucleated cell fragment originating from megakaryocytes that plays a crucial role in forming a plug to stop bleeding.

Platelet Plug

A temporary seal formed by the aggregation of platelets at the site of a blood vessel injury, representing the second phase of hemostasis.

Polycythemia

A disorder characterized by an abnormally high number of red blood cells, resulting in a high hematocrit.

Prothrombin

A plasma protein that is the inactive precursor to thrombin. It is converted to thrombin by prothrombin activator.

Rh Factor

An antigen (also known as the D antigen) that may be present on the surface of red blood cells. Its presence makes a person Rh positive, while its absence makes them Rh negative.

Thrombin

An enzyme that converts soluble fibrinogen into insoluble fibrin during the blood coagulation cascade.

Thrombus

An abnormal blood clot that forms inside an unbroken blood vessel.
